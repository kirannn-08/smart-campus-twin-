
The proposed project aims to develop an intelligent smart campus energy management system that functions as a real-time digital twin of a hybrid renewable energy infrastructure integrating solar power generation and conventional grid electricity. The system is designed to demonstrate how modern digital technologies, artificial intelligence, Internet of Things (IoT) infrastructure, and immersive visualization platforms can be combined to monitor, predict, optimize, and manage energy consumption across a campus environment. The primary goal of the project is to simulate and showcase how an intelligent energy management framework can improve renewable energy utilization, reduce reliance on grid electricity, detect abnormal consumption patterns, and provide comprehensive real-time monitoring of energy distribution across different rooms and facilities within a campus.

At its core, the system represents a conceptual campus electrical network consisting of multiple buildings, classrooms, laboratories, offices, and shared facilities, each with varying energy requirements throughout the day. During the initial development stage of the project, all energy data will be generated synthetically to simulate realistic operational behavior. The system will emulate an IoT-based infrastructure where virtual sensors produce time-series data representing solar energy generation, grid power availability, and room-level electricity consumption. This synthetic dataset will mimic real-world variations caused by factors such as time-of-day demand fluctuations, building occupancy levels, equipment usage in laboratories, lighting systems, and environmental effects on solar power generation. By generating realistic time-series energy data, the project can effectively simulate a real campus energy ecosystem while enabling rapid experimentation with predictive analytics and intelligent control strategies.

The simulated IoT layer will act as the data acquisition foundation of the system. In a real-world deployment, energy monitoring devices placed in different rooms or electrical distribution panels would continuously measure parameters such as voltage, current, power consumption, and load characteristics. In the prototype phase, however, synthetic data generation algorithms will simulate these readings in real time. The generated dataset will include multiple variables such as solar energy output, grid power availability, building-level demand, room-level consumption patterns, and temporal features like time, day, and usage cycles. This synthetic data stream will serve as the input for the machine learning models responsible for forecasting energy demand and detecting anomalies in the energy network.

A key component of the system is the implementation of advanced machine learning models capable of analyzing complex time-series energy data. Instead of relying on older recurrent neural network architectures such as traditional LSTMs, the project will employ modern deep learning architectures specifically designed for time-series forecasting and multivariate system modeling. Transformer-based neural networks such as Temporal Fusion Transformers (TFT) and other state-of-the-art forecasting architectures will be explored due to their superior ability to capture long-term temporal dependencies and nonlinear relationships between multiple system variables. These architectures leverage attention mechanisms that allow the model to dynamically focus on relevant portions of historical data while making predictions about future energy demand.

The neural network architecture will be designed to process multivariate input features including simulated solar power generation levels, grid electricity availability, historical room-level energy consumption, time-of-day indicators, and operational patterns across campus facilities. The input layer of the model will receive these multivariate time-series signals and feed them into a sequence of temporal processing layers designed to extract meaningful patterns from the data. Temporal convolution layers may be used initially to capture short-term fluctuations in energy usage, followed by transformer-based attention blocks that model long-range dependencies across multiple time horizons. Multi-head attention mechanisms will enable the network to analyze relationships between different energy variables simultaneously while identifying important temporal patterns such as peak demand periods or solar generation fluctuations.

To support deeper learning and improved model stability, the architecture may incorporate residual connections, layer normalization, and nonlinear activation functions such as GELU or ReLU within the feedforward layers. These components allow the neural network to learn complex hierarchical representations of the energy system while preventing issues such as vanishing gradients during training. Dropout regularization layers will also be included to reduce overfitting and ensure the model generalizes effectively when trained on synthetic datasets. The final output layer of the neural network will generate short-term forecasts of campus energy demand, which will then be used by the intelligent energy controller to make optimized decisions regarding power allocation.

At the heart of the system lies the intelligent energy controller responsible for managing the interaction between solar power generation and grid electricity supply. The controller will continuously analyze both real-time energy data and machine learning predictions to determine the most efficient energy source for powering campus facilities. The control strategy will prioritize solar power whenever sufficient generation is available, ensuring maximum utilization of renewable energy resources. When solar energy production is insufficient to meet the predicted demand, the controller will automatically supplement the remaining energy requirement using grid electricity. This hybrid allocation mechanism ensures that the system maintains stable power availability while minimizing reliance on non-renewable energy sources.

In addition to managing energy sources, the controller will also simulate intelligent distribution of power across campus rooms and facilities. By leveraging demand predictions generated by the machine learning models, the system can dynamically allocate available energy resources based on anticipated consumption patterns. For example, laboratories operating heavy electrical equipment during certain hours may be allocated higher priority compared to unused classrooms. This simulated smart allocation mechanism demonstrates how predictive analytics can enable more efficient energy distribution in large-scale environments.

Another important component of the system is anomaly detection, which aims to identify unusual energy usage patterns or potential system faults. Rather than relying solely on simple threshold-based monitoring, the project will utilize advanced machine learning approaches such as deep autoencoders, variational autoencoders, or ensemble-based anomaly detection algorithms. These models will learn the normal behavior of the campus energy network and detect deviations from expected patterns. Such anomalies may indicate issues such as malfunctioning equipment, abnormal energy spikes, inefficient electrical usage, or unauthorized energy consumption. When an anomaly is detected, the system will generate alerts that can be displayed on the monitoring dashboard, allowing users to investigate and respond to potential problems.

To provide a comprehensive and intuitive monitoring experience, the entire system will be represented as a real-time digital twin of the campus energy infrastructure. A digital twin is a virtual representation of a physical system that continuously updates based on real-time data inputs. In this project, the digital twin will visually simulate the energy ecosystem of the campus, illustrating how electricity flows from solar panels and grid sources through a central controller and into various buildings and rooms. The digital twin will dynamically update its state based on the simulated IoT data streams and outputs from the machine learning models. This virtual representation allows users to observe system behavior, energy distribution patterns, and overall performance in an interactive and visually engaging manner.

The visualization layer of the project will be implemented as a modern web-based platform designed to deliver an immersive user experience. The system will feature an interactive 3D landing page that introduces the concept of intelligent campus energy management. The landing page will use scroll-based animations and dynamic visual effects to illustrate the generation of solar energy, the flow of electricity through campus infrastructure, and the integration of artificial intelligence in managing energy distribution. A three-dimensional representation of the campus environment will be displayed, with animated energy flows connecting solar panels, energy controllers, and campus buildings. These visualizations will help communicate complex system concepts in an intuitive and engaging way.

After the landing page, users will be able to navigate to a comprehensive analytics dashboard that serves as the operational interface for the digital twin. The dashboard will display real-time system metrics such as solar power generation, grid electricity consumption, total campus energy demand, and the percentage of energy supplied by renewable sources. Detailed room-level analytics will show how much power is consumed by individual classrooms, laboratories, or facilities. Interactive charts and graphs will visualize energy consumption trends, demand forecasts generated by the neural network models, and historical energy usage patterns. Additional visual indicators will highlight anomalies or system alerts detected by the anomaly detection models. The dashboard will combine data analytics with interactive 3D visualization to provide a complete overview of the campus energy system.

Once the machine learning models have been successfully trained and validated using synthetic datasets, the project will progress to a hardware prototype implementation using ESP32 microcontrollers. The ESP32 devices will function as physical IoT nodes capable of measuring electrical parameters and transmitting real-time data to the backend system. Each ESP32 module will represent a monitoring unit installed in a campus room or electrical distribution point. These devices can be connected to energy monitoring sensors such as ACS712 current sensors or INA219 power monitoring modules to measure electrical current, voltage, and power consumption.

The ESP32 firmware will be programmed to periodically read sensor values, convert them into meaningful electrical measurements, and transmit the data to a backend server using Wi-Fi connectivity. Communication protocols such as HTTP or MQTT can be used to send structured JSON data packets containing room identifiers, sensor readings, and timestamps. The backend server will receive this data and store it in a database, where it will be processed by the machine learning models and integrated into the digital twin visualization system. By streaming real-time sensor data from ESP32 devices, the hardware prototype demonstrates how the simulated system can transition into a real IoT-enabled energy monitoring infrastructure.

The development of the hardware prototype will follow several steps. First, the ESP32 microcontroller will be configured using a development environment such as Arduino IDE or PlatformIO. Next, energy monitoring sensors will be connected to the ESP32's analog or I2C pins to measure electrical parameters. The firmware will then be developed to read sensor inputs, perform necessary calibration and conversion calculations, and transmit the collected data over Wi-Fi. Finally, the backend API endpoints will be configured to receive and store incoming sensor data, allowing the digital twin and analytics dashboard to update dynamically.

In summary, this project presents a comprehensive prototype of a smart campus energy management ecosystem that integrates synthetic IoT data simulation, advanced deep learning models for energy forecasting, intelligent hybrid energy control strategies, anomaly detection analytics, digital twin visualization, and real-world IoT hardware integration using ESP32 devices. By combining artificial intelligence, renewable energy technologies, IoT infrastructure, and immersive web-based visualization, the system demonstrates how modern digital solutions can transform energy monitoring and management in large-scale environments. The project highlights the potential of intelligent energy systems to improve renewable energy utilization, reduce operational inefficiencies, detect faults proactively, and provide transparent real-time insights into complex energy networks.
